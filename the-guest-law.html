A Story Told Everywhere

The Guest-Law

They tell this story differently depending on where you're born, but they always tell it.

Every world that has ever had a door has a version of the Guest-Law. Every people who have ever lit a fire against the dark have argued about its terms. The details change — the shape of the door, the color of the fire, the name of the season it happens in — but the law underneath is always the same, and so is the story about the one time it broke.

Here is the shape of the law, in the plainest telling anyone agrees on:

A stranger may come to your door out of the dark and ask for shelter, and you must give it, because the dark does not care whether you like the shape of the one asking. You may give them warmth. You may give them water, or bread, or whatever your people call the thing that means the same as both. You may give them a name to call you by, and take theirs in return, if they'll part with it. But you must not ask them to stay, and they must not offer to. Before the fire dies, or the tide turns, or the light comes back over the hill — whichever marks the edge of night in your particular corner of everywhere — the stranger goes back out into the dark they came from, and you close the door behind them, and that is how both of you stay safe from each other.

It is a good law. It has kept a great many people alive, on a great many worlds, for a great many centuries. It exists because strangers are not safe, and doors are not safe, and the space between one life and another is exactly as dangerous as it has always been, no matter how far anyone has traveled to get away from saying so.

But every telling of the Guest-Law carries, folded inside it like a second, smaller story, the account of the time it broke. Not once. Not in one place. In every place. Every culture that has ever written down or sung or otherwise kept this law has also kept, right alongside it, the story of the guest who did not leave before morning.

They don't agree on what to call it. In some tellings it's a tragedy — a warning, the reason a particular well went dry or a particular house still stands empty. In others it's the best thing that ever happened to the people who tell it, the founding story of an entire family line, proof that the rule was made to be broken by exactly one person, at exactly the right door. A few tellings refuse to say whether it was good or bad at all, and just tell you it happened, the way you'd tell someone it rained.

What none of them do, in all the languages this story has ever been dressed in, is call it impossible. That's the part worth noticing. A law this old, this widely kept, this thoroughly agreed upon — and not one people, anywhere, in any telling, has ever claimed it cannot be broken. Only that it shouldn't be. Only that it usually isn't.

A Telling From the Water

In one telling, the door is a door — an actual, ordinary, wooden thing on an actual, ordinary house, and the guest comes up from the water at low tide, because that's where guests come from in a telling like that, someone who lives in the space between air and sea and isn't supposed to want to breathe the same room as you for longer than one changing of the tide. The story goes that the housekeeper left a lamp burning past the turn of the water, on purpose, more than once, and that eventually the sea stopped asking for its guest back.

A Telling From the Road

In another telling there is no door at all, only a road, and the guest is the road itself — or someone who has walked it so long he has forgotten which parts of himself are still separate from it, a wanderer whose whole purpose is to never arrive anywhere long enough to need a door. In that version, the law isn't about shelter. It's about distance. The guest is supposed to keep moving, because a road that stops being walked stops being a road, and something in the old telling insists he'll cease to exist the moment he chooses to stay in one place. The story goes that someone walked out to meet him halfway, instead of waiting for him to come the rest of the way in, and that this — meeting him on the road rather than asking him through a door — was somehow enough to satisfy a law that had never once considered the possibility of being met partway.

A Telling From the Garden

In a third telling, the guest arrives the way a seed arrives — carried in, planted in soil that was never meant to hold it, tended by someone who wasn't supposed to get attached to a thing that was only ever passing through on its way to becoming something else, somewhere else. The law in that version says the guest must be transplanted out before the roots take hold, because roots that take hold in the wrong ground choke out whatever was growing there first. The story goes that the roots took hold anyway, and that — against every rule anyone had ever written about what belongs to what soil — the garden did not choke. It just got a little more crowded, and a little more alive.

Bridge, tide, root. Wanderer, watcher, gardener. None of these people ever met each other. None of their worlds share a sky, a language, or a single grain of the same soil. And yet if you set their stories side by side — which almost no one ever does, because why would you, they belong to different corners of everywhere — you'd notice they are not three stories. They are one story, worn into three different shapes by three different mouths, the way water wears the same shape into whatever rock happens to be in its way.

Scholars who study this sort of thing — and there are a surprising number of them, on a surprising number of worlds, given how little money there is in it — have never agreed on why the Guest-Law shows up everywhere, nearly identical, among peoples who could not possibly have compared notes. Some say it's just what happens when any two kinds of creature capable of loneliness eventually bump into each other: the law and its exception get invented independently, over and over, the way wheels get invented independently, because a story this useful is bound to occur to anyone who's ever needed it. Others say something stranger — that the story is less like a wheel and more like a constellation, meaningless shapes we all happen to draw the same lines between, because the stars we're looking at are, in some sense, too large for any one telling to hold — the same stars.

Nobody has proven either theory. Nobody has really tried very hard, if you want the truth of it — some questions are more useful unanswered, and this may be one of them.

Here is what is agreed on, in every telling, on every world that keeps a version of this law: the guest who stays is never the same afterward, and neither is the house. Something that was built to be closed against the dark gets built a little differently once it's had a stranger sleep past morning inside it. The door doesn't stop being a door. It just remembers, from then on, that it was built to open as much as it was built to close, and it starts doing both a little more easily than it used to.

This is why, on almost every world where people still tell stories before sleep, there is some version of this one. Not because it is only a story. Because somewhere, on more evenings than anyone keeps count of, someone leaves the door unlatched on purpose, and doesn't tell anyone they've done it, and lies awake afterward wondering if the oldest law in the world will notice, or mind, or come to collect what it's owed.

It hasn't yet. As far as anyone has ever managed to find out.

So the lamp stays lit a little past the turning tide. The road gets met halfway more often than the old law accounts for. The garden keeps getting a little more crowded, and a little more alive.

And somewhere — everywhere, if the scholars arguing about constellations happen to be right — the story gets told again tonight, in whatever shape the listener needs it to take, right up until the part where the guest is supposed to leave.

Most tellings still end there, the way they're supposed to.

Not all of them do anymore.
